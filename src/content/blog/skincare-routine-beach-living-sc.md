Living on the Grand Strand is a gift. I moved here because I love the ocean, the pace, the light. But after years of doing facials on coastal skin, I can tell you something that most generic skincare advice completely misses: *beach life is hard on your face.*

Not in a "you should move inland" way — obviously don't do that. But in a "your routine probably needs a few specific tweaks" way. Here's what I see all the time with Myrtle Beach skincare clients, and how to fix it.

## The Three Coastal Skin Enemies

**UV exposure — and it's worse than you think.** South Carolina ranks among the highest states for UV index. Even on overcast days, up to 80% of UV rays reach your skin. If you're spending any time outside — walking the beach, sitting on a porch, driving with the windows down — you're accumulating sun damage whether you can feel it or not. This shows up as uneven tone, dark spots, and premature aging that creeps up slowly until one day you catch yourself in bright light and wonder when that happened.

**Salt air dehydration.** Salt is hygroscopic — it pulls moisture out of things, including your skin. Spending time near the ocean, with salt particles in the air, slowly draws water from your skin's surface. A lot of my Grand Strand clients come in thinking they have oily skin when they actually have dehydrated skin that's overproducing oil to compensate. The solution isn't to dry out your skin more. It's to hydrate it properly.

**Humidity that tricks you.** This one is sneaky. High humidity in summer makes your skin *feel* moisturized, so a lot of people skip moisturizer. But atmospheric humidity isn't the same as your skin's actual hydration levels — and skipping moisturizer disrupts your barrier function over time.

## Your Beach Skincare Morning Routine

Start with a gentle cleanser — nothing stripping. If your cleanser makes your skin feel tight after rinsing, it's too harsh for coastal skin (or any skin, honestly).

Layer on an antioxidant serum next, ideally vitamin C. This is your first line of defense against UV-induced free radical damage. It doesn't replace sunscreen, but it makes your sunscreen work better and protects against the oxidative stress of sun exposure.

Then moisturizer. Yes, even in July. Even if it's humid. A lightweight gel moisturizer is fine — it seals in hydration without feeling heavy.

Then SPF 30 or higher, every single morning, as the last step. Not just on beach days. Every. Day. This is the one product I will never stop emphasizing.

## Your Beach Skincare Evening Routine

Double cleanse. This is especially important if you're wearing sunscreen — which you should be. Your regular cleanser often doesn't fully break down SPF and salt residue. Start with a cleansing oil or balm to dissolve it, then follow with your regular cleanser. Your skin will feel completely different afterward.

In the evening, use a hydrating serum (hyaluronic acid is great for pulling moisture into the skin) and follow with a richer moisturizer than you think you need. The evening is when your skin repairs itself, and it needs resources to do that.

## Seasonal Shifts on the Grand Strand

Summer means lighter textures but higher UV vigilance. Reapply sunscreen if you're outside for more than a couple of hours.

Winter here is mild compared to most of the country, but the wind picks up and adds a drying factor on top of the UV that never really goes away. Bump up your moisturizer weight in the cooler months and don't let the cloudy skies convince you to skip SPF.
